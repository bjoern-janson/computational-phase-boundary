# Cross-Domain Transfer: Process Taxonomies

One of the strongest signals observed during the development of Computational Phase Boundaries (CPB) was an unexpected transfer to an unrelated system: an independently developed music playlist taxonomy.

Importantly, this is **not evidence that CPB is correct**. Instead, it provides evidence that the underlying abstraction may capture a more general organizing principle than originally intended.

---

## The Observation

The playlist taxonomy had been developed long before CPB and was already highly stable.

Rather than classifying songs by genre, BPM, or mood, it classified them by the **cognitive trajectories** they induced:

* **Velocity** — sustained forward movement
* **Dreamstate** — stable internal representation
* **Expansion** — widening accessible possibilities
* **Ascension** — transition into a qualitatively different state
* **Overdrive** — exploitation and tactical execution
* **Festival** — collective amplification and synchronization

Despite remaining imperfect, the taxonomy required remarkably few corrections over hundreds of classified tracks.

---

## What CPB Changed

CPB did not introduce new playlist categories.

Instead, it changed the **axis of classification**.

The question shifted from:

> **"What does this song feel like?"**

to:

> **"What transformation does this song induce in the dynamics of cognition?"**

The result was a noticeably cleaner separation between categories that previously occupied similar aesthetic territory.

For example:

### Dreamstate

Rather than simply describing atmospheric or emotional music, Dreamstate became:

> **Stabilizes an internal representational landscape, allowing hidden structure to emerge without requiring forward traversal.**

### Expansion

Instead of "large" or "epic" sounding music:

> **Increases the accessible cognitive possibility space.**

### Velocity

Not:

> "Fast."

Instead:

> **Produces sustained movement through cognitive space.**

### Ascension

Not:

> "Emotional climax."

Instead:

> **Induces the perception of crossing into a qualitatively different cognitive regime.**

The categories became **dynamical**, not aesthetic.

---

## The Structural Parallel

The interesting observation is not that music and computation are equivalent.

Rather, both systems underwent the same conceptual transition.

Traditional classification:

```text
Song → Genre
Technology → Category
```

Process classification:

```text
Thing
↓
Transformation it induces
↓
Classification
```

The object becomes secondary.

The induced transformation becomes primary.

---

## Playlist Taxonomy

The playlist taxonomy asks:

> **How does this piece of music transform the trajectory of a human mind?**

---

## Computational Phase Boundaries

CPB asks:

> **How does this technology transform the trajectory of future search?**

---

## Shared Structure

| Playlist Taxonomy            | Computational Phase Boundaries  |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Human cognitive trajectory   | Technological search trajectory |
| Internal exploration         | External exploration            |
| Mental state transitions     | Computational state transitions |
| Representation of experience | Representation of possibility   |

The connection is not based on content.

It is based on the **kind of abstraction** being used.

Both classify systems by the trajectories they induce over time.

---

## Why This Matters

Unexpected improvements in an unrelated domain are consistent with a framework capturing a more general abstraction, although such transfer alone does not establish correctness.

Applying it to another domain usually produces:

* additional ambiguity,
* more exceptions,
* increasingly complex rules.

Instead, applying the CPB lens to the playlist taxonomy appeared to produce:

* cleaner category boundaries,
* fewer ambiguous cases,
* simpler explanations,
* more operational definitions.

This is **not validation of CPB**, but it is evidence that the framework may capture a domain-general property rather than a technology-specific one.

---

## A Candidate General Principle

A possible generalization is:

> **Complex systems may be more naturally classified by how they alter the dynamics of exploration than by the objects they contain.**

This represents a shift from **object taxonomies** to **process taxonomies**.

Examples include:

* scientific theories,
* programming languages,
* interfaces,
* educational systems,
* organizations,
* technological paradigms,
* and, potentially, human cognitive states.

---

## Relationship to IICG

This observation also strengthens the conceptual bridge between Interface-Induced Computational Geometry (IICG) and CPB.

* **IICG** studies how representations reshape computational geometry.
* **CPB** studies when those geometric changes produce capability transitions.
* The playlist taxonomy can be interpreted as a phenomenological analogue, where representations reshape **cognitive geometry** rather than computational geometry.

One possible interpretation is that the three frameworks share a common representational perspective.

---

## Future Work

The strongest future test is not whether CPB explains additional technologies after the fact.

It is whether the framework consistently improves reasoning in domains where it was **not** designed to operate.

If repeatedly asking:

> **"How does this transform future exploration?"**

produces better classifications or decisions than asking:

> **"What kind of object is this?"**

then the framework may be capturing a genuinely useful domain-general abstraction rather than a technology-specific taxonomy.
